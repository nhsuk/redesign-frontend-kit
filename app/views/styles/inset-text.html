Previous research

No previous research found.

User need

As a user I NEED to identify and understand important content related to my condition or medicine (even if I don't read all the content) SO THAT I get the best outcome for my situation.

NHS need

As an NHS.UK content designer I NEED to be able to draw user attention to information that is deemed to be more important than the body copy SO THAT users are more likely to read and understand the key information for their situation.

Design and research

Notes

The colour blue is used for notices on safety signs.

Safety signage example

Design challenge

  • Design a warning callout that will work well in both contexts, for medicines and conditions.
  • Make the design feel a part of the new design system.

First iteration

Hypothesis

If we make the callout blue and use a (i) icon
Then users will notice it
Because they see it as important information.

Design decisions

  • Having the icon in a square offset left will more in keeping with other new design elements such as the Do and don’t lists.

Research findings

Leeds, pop-up research, over 20 people

  • Some users commented that "it looks like an advert". See banner blindness.

Second iteration

Hypothesis

If we remove the icon and make it less stylish
Then users are more likley read it
Because they will see it as part of the page and as not an advert.

Research findings

Leeds, pop-up research, over 10 people

The white background made it too prominent, and it wasn't clear where it belonged in a hierarchy (with other callouts).

Third iteration

Hypothesis

If we remove the white background
Then users will still notice it
Because it still stands out from the body copy.

NHS.UK usability lab, Leeds, 20th June 2018

  • 3/5 users spontaneously noticed the key point, and understood its purpose and place in hierarchy of callouts.
    “It stands out. It makes you read that piece”
    “The blue bar made me look at it”
  • However 2/5 users scrolled past it and didn’t notice until prompted.
    But these users understood it’s purpose and it’s position in a hierarchy of callouts.

NHS.UK usability lab, Sheffield, 16th July 2018

  • 3/5 users noticed the key point and understood it’s meaning and placement in the hierarchy.
  • 2 users initially missed the key point and needed prompting to see it.

Example and code snippet

{{ keypoint("

Sometimes AMD may be found during a routine eye test before you have symptoms.

") }} 
<div role="note" aria-label="Information" class="nhsuk-c-inset-text">
  <p>Sometimes AMD may be found during a routine eye test before you have symptoms.</p>
</div>

Accessibility

We have used role="note" and aria-label="Information" to inform users with screenreaders that this is supportive information and different to the body text.

Usage (rules)

When to use this component

Use the inset text component to differentiate a block of text from the content that surrounds it.

When not to use this component

Some users do not notice inset text if it’s used on complex pages or near to other visually prominent elements. For this reason, avoid using inset text as a way of highlighting very important information that users need to see.

If you need to draw attention to very important content, like health risk, use the warning callout component instead.
