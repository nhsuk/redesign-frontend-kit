What was being used

Yellow warning used on medicines

Yellow warning used on conditions

Previous research

In a usability lab, the medicines team tested their new design against the current design used on conditions pages. The outcome was that users saw the medicines design as a warning, being more ‘important’ and the (!) icon drew their attention.

Notes from the testing…

  • P1 - Liked the (!) icon – it brought my attention
  • P3 – was drawn to [these] ones, especially with the (!)
  • P3 – (!) make me feel as though it’s a must read.

Acceptance criteria

A user is able to interpret that the warning callout as being something important in comparison to the rest of the page.

User need

As a user I need to identify and understand the important content on the page (even if I don't read all the content) so that I get the best outcome for my situation and don't endanger my health.

Design and research

Notes

Yellow is the most visible colour of the spectrum.

The human eye processes yellow first. This explains why it is used for cautionary signs and emergency rescue vehicles.

Safety signage example

Design challenge

  • Design a warning callout that will work well in both contexts, for medicines and conditions.
  • Make the design feel a part of the new design system.

First iteration

Hypothesis

If we make the callout black/yellow and use a (!) icon
Then users will take notice
Because they see it as a warning and important information.

Design decisions

  • Having the icon in a square offset left will more in keeping with other new design elements such as the Do and don’t lists.

Research findings

Leeds, pop-up research x 2, over 50 people

  • Users didn’t understand it as a warning
  • Some users didn’t see it as part of the page

Second iteration

Hypothesis

If we make the yellow brighter and increase the font weight
Then users will take more notice and read it
Because it looks more like a warning

Research findings

Usability lab, Leeds, 13th June 2018

  • 3/5 five users initially missed the callout, with one indicating their attention was drawn to the heading below.
  • Most users correctly understood its importance vs other call outs and the meaning of the yellow colour as a warning.

Third iteration

Hypothesis

Previous usability testing has shown us that users scan headings.
If we add a heading
Then users will notice the callout more
Because users scan headings

If we change the background colour to yellow
Then users will notice the callout more
Because it will help it stand out from the surrounding white boxes

Research findings

Usability lab, Leeds, 20th June 2018

  • All users naturally noticed the yellow callout, and 4/5 of the users understood its meaning and place in the hierarchy.
  • “It’s a warning, but it’s not scary”
  • “The ‘Important’ heading stood out”

Usability lab, Salford, 4th July 2018

  • Overall the important box tested well, with most users understanding it where it belongs in a hierarchy.

Usability lab, Sheffield, 16th July 2018

  • All users understood the meaning of the important call out box, and where it belongs in the hierarchy. Users stated it would make them take notice of the important information.
  • All users understood the meaning behind each of these elements, and where each belonged in a hierarchy of importance.
  • “It jumps out. If I was scanning I’d go back to that.”

Accessibility

Colour contrast

The heading and background contrast ratio is 11.92:1, which exceeds AAA guidelines.

The content and background contrast ratio is 13.69:1, which exceeds AAA guidelines.

ARIA

We do not use an aria-label because it could cause confusion having the heading and aria-label set as ‘Important’.

If the heading was made editable (see future developments), we recommend adding an aria-label, eg: aria-label=”Important”. This is will help users with screen readers understand that the information is important. This will need to be tested by users with access needs.

Usage (rules)

Use the important callout to display information that is:

  • time critical
  • may have a significant health impact
  • addresses a common and/or significant misconception or patient mistake

Give warning callouts a short, clearly worded heading. Headings help people scan the page for warnings and understand what's relevant to them.

Future developments

Due to time constraints, the heading of the warning callout component in wagtail is not editable, the heading ‘Important’ is hard coded. Moving forward, we propose making the heading editable to make it more contextual.

An example of this would be on the chickenpox page…

Current design with hardcoded header

Proposed design with editable header

Research has shown us that users scan the page for keywords.

We believe that if a user was checking to see if they should send their child to school when they have chickenpox… they would scan the page for the word 'school'.

So changing the heading to ‘School, nursery or work’ would make the information more noticeable and easier to find.

At the moment this is just a hypothesis and should be tested with users.
